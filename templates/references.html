BabyQ References

This page organizes the supporting evidence for each BabyQ question. It is designed as an educational resource for expectant mothers, doulas, and midwives.

Q1: I have avoided drinking any alcohol during my pregnancy.

Why this matters: No safe amount of alcohol has been established in pregnancy. Even low levels can impair brain development and increase risks of miscarriage, preterm birth, and fetal alcohol spectrum disorders (FASD).

  • AAP Clinical Report on FASD
  • Popova et al., Nat Rev Dis Primers, 2023
  • Lees et al., Am J Psychiatry, 2020
  • May et al., JAMA, 2018
  • Flak et al., Meta-analysis, 2014
  • CDC: Alcohol and Pregnancy
  • ACOG: Alcohol and Pregnancy FAQ
Q2: I have not smoked during my pregnancy.

Why this matters: Smoking increases the risk of miscarriage, stillbirth, preterm birth, and sudden infant death syndrome (SIDS). Nicotine replacement therapy (NRT) is safer than continued smoking and can be used under medical guidance.

  • Liu et al., PLoS Med, 2020
  • Pineles et al., Am J Epidemiol, 2014
  • Coleman et al., NEJM, 2012 (SNAP trial)
  • Cochrane Review: Pharmacological cessation
  • ACOG: Smoking During Pregnancy
Q3: I have a restful 7 hours sleep at least 5 nights per week.

Why this matters: Sleep less than 7 hours increases risks of preterm birth, cesarean delivery, and hypertension. Quality rest supports maternal health, labor outcomes, and infant growth.

  • Lee & Gay, Am J Obstet Gynecol, 2004
  • Okun et al., Sleep, 2011
  • Xu et al., BMC Pregnancy Childbirth, 2020
  • Young-Lin et al., eBioMedicine, 2025
Q4: I attend all scheduled prenatal checkups (or have a plan to attend them).

Why this matters: Regular prenatal visits reduce risks of maternal and infant mortality, allow early detection of complications, and provide preventive care such as screenings and vaccinations.

  • Huang et al., Midwifery, 2018
  • Alexander & Kotelchuck, Public Health Rep, 2001
  • Turrentine et al., J Womens Health, 2024
  • ACOG Clinical Consensus on Tailored Care, 2025
Q5: I perform moderate-intensity physical activity for at least 150 minutes per week.

Why this matters: Exercise lowers the risk of gestational diabetes, preeclampsia, and excessive weight gain. Occupational standing ≠ exercise — leisure activity is what matters.

  • ACOG Committee Opinion 804: Exercise
  • Magro-Malosso et al., Acta Obstet Gynecol Scand, 2017
  • Cai et al., Am J Obstet Gynecol, 2019 (Occupational risks)
  • Davenport et al., Can Guideline, 2018
Q6: I eat at least two helpings of protein rich foods per day.

Why this matters: Adequate protein supports fetal growth, maternal blood supply, and placental health. Needs rise in the 2nd and 3rd trimesters.

  • Elango & Ball, Adv Nutr, 2016
  • Stephens et al., J Nutr, 2015
  • ACOG Nutrition During Pregnancy
Q7: I limit added sugar (like soda, candy, desserts) to less than once per day.

Why this matters: High sugar intake raises risks of gestational diabetes, excessive weight gain, and childhood obesity. Sugary beverages are especially harmful.

  • Zhang et al., Diabetes Care, 2006
  • Hofmeyr et al., Cochrane Review, 2018
  • CDC: Gestational Diabetes
Q8: Over the past two weeks, I have felt mostly hopeful, positive, and able to enjoy daily activities.

Why this matters: Depression in pregnancy increases risks of preterm birth, low birth weight, and postpartum depression. Early identification and treatment are protective.

  • Woody et al., J Affect Disord, 2017
  • Grigoriadis et al., J Clin Psychiatry, 2013
  • Grote et al., Arch Gen Psychiatry, 2010
  • APA/ACOG Guidelines on Depression in Pregnancy
Q9: I eat 2 to 3 servings of vegetables at least 5 days a week.

Why this matters: Vegetables provide folate, iron, vitamin K, and antioxidants crucial for fetal development and preeclampsia prevention. Leafy greens and cruciferous vegetables are especially protective.

  • WHO Technical Report on Diet, 2003
  • Haugen et al., MoBa Cohort, 2008
  • Knudsen et al., Eur J Clin Nutr, 2008
Q10: I feel adequate support from my partner, family, and friends.

Why this matters: Strong social support reduces risks of preterm birth, low birth weight, and postpartum depression. Lack of support elevates stress hormones harmful to pregnancy.

  • Bisetegn et al., Reprod Health, 2021
  • Müller et al., BMC Pregnancy Childbirth, 2023
  • Prost et al., Lancet, 2013
Q11: I manage stress and anxiety well, feeling overwhelmed less than once per week.

Why this matters: Frequent stress (>1x/week) doubles the risk of preterm birth. Effective coping and stress management protect maternal health and fetal growth.

  • Dole et al., Am J Epidemiol, 2003
  • Staneva et al., Women Birth, 2015
  • Buss et al., PNAS, 2012
Q12: I have had a dental checkup and cleaning within the past 6 months (or have one scheduled).

Why this matters: Periodontal disease is linked to preterm birth and preeclampsia. Preventive dental care is safe in pregnancy and helps reduce systemic inflammation.

  • Bobetsis et al., Periodontol 2000, 2020
  • Michalowicz et al., NEJM, 2006
  • ACOG: Oral Health in Pregnancy
Q13: I take a daily prenatal vitamin that contains at least 400 mcg folic acid.

Why this matters: Adequate folic acid prevents neural tube defects by 50–70%. Prenatals also provide iron, vitamin D, iodine, and DHA that support maternal and fetal health.

  • Czeizel & Dudás, NEJM, 1992
  • Berry et al., NEJM, 1999
  • Cochrane Review: Folate & Birth Defects, 2015
  • CDC: Folic Acid Recommendations
Q14: I consume at least one serving of dairy daily (milk, cheese, or yogurt).

Why this matters: Dairy provides calcium, protein, and probiotics. Adequate calcium prevents maternal bone loss and supports fetal skeletal growth. Fermented dairy reduces risk of preeclampsia and gestational diabetes.

  • Hacker et al., Nutr Rev, 2012
  • Hofmeyr et al., Cochrane Review, 2018
  • Kriss et al., Matern Child Nutr, 2018
Q15: I regularly eat fiber-rich whole grains.

Why this matters: Whole grains reduce risks of gestational diabetes, preterm birth, and excessive maternal weight gain. Fiber also supports digestive health and improves microbiome diversity.

  • Zhang et al., Diabetes Care, 2006
  • Hofmeyr et al., Cochrane Review, 2018
  • Fazzi et al., Syst Rev, 2017
